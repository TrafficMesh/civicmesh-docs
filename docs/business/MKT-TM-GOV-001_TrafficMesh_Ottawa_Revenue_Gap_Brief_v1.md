# 1. The Problem — Ottawa's $38.7M Revenue Gap


| Executive Summary
Ontario's November 2025 automated speed enforcement ban eliminated $27.8M in Ottawa speed camera revenue and $10.9M in red light camera revenue — $38.7M annually. This is not a policy disagreement. It is a fiscal reality that must be addressed in the next budget cycle. |

# 2. The Solution — TrafficMesh Mobile Enforcement


| What it is | A distributed enforcement platform that turns existing city fleet vehicles — buses, plows, bylaw vehicles, garbage trucks — into mobile enforcement nodes. No new infrastructure. No fixed cameras. Cameras that move cannot be avoided. |
|---|---|
| Why it is not covered by the ASE ban | Ontario's ban covers fixed, roadside automated enforcement systems. TrafficMesh is vehicle-mounted and requires human officer review before every citation — the same standard as an officer writing a ticket by hand. Legal opinion: [REG-TM-001 — available on request]. |
| NYC MTA precedent | New York's bus-mounted ACE programme issued 76.4% of all bus lane and bus stop violations in December 2024. $225M in fines over 24 months. Bus speeds increased 5%, collisions reduced 20%. Ottawa can implement the same model. |
| Revenue projection for Ottawa | OC Transpo pilot (30 buses, 6 months): $225,000–$900,000. City fleet Phase 2 (200 vehicles): $1.4M–$4.8M annually. Full Ottawa deployment (500+ vehicles): $5.8M–$19.2M annually. Conservative planning estimate: $3M–$5M annually by Year 2. |


# 3. Why TrafficMesh Is Politically Defensible


| Political Concern | Answer |
|---|---|
| 'Is this just another cash grab?' | No — TrafficMesh is a community dividend programme. Ottawa residents who opt in their own dashcams earn a share of enforcement revenue. The community profits from the safety network they participate in. |
| 'Will it face the same backlash as ASE cameras?' | Fixed cameras were banned because the community had no stake in the outcome. TrafficMesh communities share in the revenue. When a politician proposes banning it, they propose taking money out of residents' pockets. |
| 'Is there a human reviewing these tickets?' | Every citation requires a trained officer to review the footage and sign off with their credentials. Technically enforced — no bypass possible. |
| 'Who owns the data?' | The City of Ottawa owns all enforcement data. Not a private company. Not a US corporation. Open source, auditable, and hosted in Canada. |
