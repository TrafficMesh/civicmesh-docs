# 1. The Displacement Effect — Why Fixed Cameras Don't Change Behaviour


| The problem | Fixed speed cameras create a displacement effect: violation rates drop to near-zero within 50 metres of the camera and return to baseline within 200 metres. Every GPS navigation app flags their location within days of installation. The behaviour does not change — only its location changes. |
|---|---|
| The evidence | Studies of Ontario's ASE programme consistently showed violation reduction at camera locations with minimal corridor-wide safety improvement. This is the same finding that informed the Ford government's political decision to ban them — the safety rationale was weaker than the revenue rationale. |
| The conclusion | Fixed enforcement generates revenue but does not create generalized driver behaviour change. This is why Ottawa spent $80M on ASE cameras and still had the same road safety challenges. |


# 2. Generalized Deterrence — How Mobile Enforcement Changes Behaviour


| The mechanism | When drivers don't know which vehicle is an enforcement node, rational behaviour is to comply everywhere rather than nowhere. The same mechanism makes police presence effective even when the car is not actively watching — the possibility of observation changes behaviour more than certainty of observation at one known point. |
|---|---|
| NYC ACE evidence | NYC MTA ACE bus-mounted cameras reduced collisions on bus corridors by 20% — not just violations at specific camera locations but actual collisions across the entire route. This is evidence of generalized behaviour change, not just point enforcement. |
| The safety argument | TrafficMesh is primarily a deterrence tool. The revenue is a byproduct of compliance — and compliance is the actual goal. This framing matters in public consultation: 'we are not trying to catch people, we are trying to change behaviour.' |


# 3. Community Revenue Share as Safety Infrastructure Funding


| The virtuous cycle | Citation revenue → CCSC member patronage → more civilian opt-in → more network density → better deterrence → fewer violations → safer roads. The revenue share is not just a political sweetener — it is the mechanism that builds the network density that creates the deterrence. |
|---|---|
| Comparison to fixed cameras | Fixed cameras: revenue extracted from community → government general fund → political resistance. TrafficMesh: revenue generated by community → flows back to community → political constituency for expansion. |
| The policy recommendation | Frame TrafficMesh as road safety infrastructure, funded by the violations it prevents, owned by the community it protects. Revenue is the evidence that it is working. |
