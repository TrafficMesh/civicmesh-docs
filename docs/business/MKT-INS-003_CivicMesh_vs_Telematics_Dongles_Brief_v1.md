# 1. The Self-Reported Telematics Problem


| Issue | Telematics Dongle | CivicMesh |
|---|---|---|
| Data source | Policyholder's own vehicle — incentivized to modify behaviour | Third-party enforcement infrastructure — no incentive to modify behaviour |
| Behaviour modification | Studies show 40–60% of drivers modify their behaviour when they know the dongle is active | Nodes are on city vehicles, civilian dashcams, delivery trucks — the driver doesn't know which vehicles are active |
| Verification | Self-reported by insurer's own device — no independent verification | Evidence-grade data from the same infrastructure used for enforcement citations |
| Data coverage | Only the insured vehicle — no context about the roads they use | Both the driver's behaviour AND the risk of the corridors they drive |
| Fraud potential | Driver can remove/cover dongle during high-risk driving | Not applicable — driver has no visibility into which vehicles carry nodes |
| Privacy optics | Driver knows their insurer is tracking them — compliance pressure | Driver consents once and the monitoring is passive — less friction and resentment |


# 2. The UBI Backbone Advantage


| What CivicMesh enables | A UBI product that prices on actual validated behaviour — not on self-reported behaviour during a monitored period. The policyholder who opts in provides 12 months of continuous, third-party-verified behaviour data, not a 3-month monitored period where they know they're being assessed. |
|---|---|
| The sample size advantage | By Year 3, CivicMesh will have 24+ months of behaviour data for 10,000+ Ottawa opted-in drivers. This is the largest verified driving behaviour dataset in Ottawa. No insurer's own telematics programme comes close. |
| The anti-discrimination advantage | CivicMesh behaviour scoring uses only validated behaviour metrics — not demographic proxies. The equity audit tool (DC-CM-APP-010) flags any correlation between risk scores and protected characteristics. |
